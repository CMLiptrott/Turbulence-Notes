An orthogonal tensor is a linear transformation for which the transformed vectors preserve their lengths and angles. Let $\mathbf{Q}$ denote an orthogonal tensor. Then, by definition, $$
|\mathbf{Q}\mathbf{a}|=\mathbf{a},\quad \text{ and }\quad \cos(\mathbf{Q}\mathbf{a}, \mathbf{Q}\mathbf{b}) = \cos(\mathbf{a}, \mathbf{b}).
$$So, $$
(\mathbf{Q}\mathbf{a})\cdot(\mathbf{Q}\mathbf{b})= |\mathbf{Q}\mathbf{a}||\mathbf{Q}\mathbf{b}|\cos(\mathbf{Q}\mathbf{a},\mathbf{Q}\mathbf{b}) = |\mathbf{a}||\mathbf{a}|\cos(\mathbf{a},\mathbf{b}) = \mathbf{a}\cdot \mathbf{b}
$$
By the definition of tensor transpose, $$
(\mathbf{Q}\mathbf{a})\cdot(\mathbf{Q}\mathbf{b}) = \mathbf{b}\cdot \mathbf{Q}^T(\mathbf{Q}\mathbf{a}).
$$Thus $$
\begin{align}
\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b} &=\mathbf{b}\cdot \mathbf{Q}^T\mathbf{Q}\mathbf{a}  \\
\Rightarrow\mathbf{b}\cdot \mathbf{I}\mathbf{a}&=\mathbf{b}\cdot \mathbf{Q}^T\mathbf{Q}\mathbf{a}  \\
\Rightarrow \mathbf{b}\cdot[\mathbf{I}&-\mathbf{Q}^T\mathbf{Q}]\mathbf{a}=\mathbf{0} 
\end{align}
$$
Since $\mathbf{a}$ and $\mathbf{b}$ are arbitrary, $$
\mathbf{Q}^T\mathbf{Q}=\mathbf{I}.
$$
Claim: The transpose of an orthogonal tensor is also orthogonal:
Proof: We have shown that if $\mathbf{Q}$ is orthogonal, then $\mathbf{Q}^T\mathbf{Q}=\mathbf{I}.$ Take the transpose of $\mathbf{Q}^T\mathbf{Q}$:$$
 (\mathbf{Q}^T\mathbf{Q})^T = (\mathbf{Q}^T)^T\mathbf{Q}^T = \mathbf{Q}\mathbf{Q}^T,
$$ and $$
 (\mathbf{Q}^T\mathbf{Q})^T = \mathbf{I}^T = \mathbf{I}.
$$
Hence $$
\mathbf{Q}\mathbf{Q}^T=\mathbf{I}.
$$Now, $\mathbf{Q}^T$ is orthogonal if $\mathbf{Q}^T\mathbf{a}\cdot \mathbf{Q}^T\mathbf{b}=\mathbf{a}\cdot \mathbf{b}$ by definition. We have   
$$
\mathbf{Q}^T\mathbf{a}\cdot \mathbf{Q}^T\mathbf{b} = \mathbf{b}\cdot (\mathbf{Q}^T)^T\mathbf{Q}^T\mathbf{a} = \mathbf{b}\cdot \mathbf{Q}\mathbf{Q}^T\mathbf{a}=\mathbf{b}\cdot \mathbf{I}\mathbf{a}=\mathbf{b}\cdot \mathbf{a}
$$
where our derived identity has been substituted. Hence $\mathbf{Q}^T$ is orthogonal.
