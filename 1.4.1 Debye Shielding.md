Plasmas can shield out electrostatic potentials applied to it. Imagine two charged balls dropped into a plasma connected to a battery (see figure below). The balls attract particles of opposite charge. Very quickly a cloud of ions would surround the negative charge and a cloud of electrons would surround the positive charge. If the plasma were cold and there were no thermal motions, the number of charges in the ball would equal the number of charges in the surrounding ion/electron cloud, and no electric field would be present outside the clouds. The shielding would be perfect. 

If there were finite temperature, the particles at the edge of the cloud, where the electric filed is weakest, would have enough thermal energy to escape from the electrostatic [[Potential Well|potential well]]. The edge of the shell then appears where the potential energy is approximately equal to the thermal energy, $KT$, of the particles. The shielding is incomplete. Potentials of the order $\frac{KT}{e}$ can leak into the plasma and cause finite electric fields to exist there.
