The dyadic product of $\mathbf{a}$ and $\mathbf{b}$ denoted by $\mathbf{ab}$ is defined ot be the transformation, which transforms any $\mathbf{c}$ according to the rule:$$
(\mathbf{ab})\mathbf{c}=\mathbf{a}(\mathbf{b}\cdot \mathbf{c}).
$$
Claim: $\mathbf{ab}$ is a tensor.
Proof: For any $\mathbf{c}$, $\mathbf{d}$, $\alpha$, $\beta$, we have $$
\begin{align*}
(\mathbf{a}\mathbf{b})(\alpha \mathbf{c}+\beta \mathbf{d})&=\mathbf{a}[\mathbf{b}\cdot [\alpha \mathbf{c}+\beta \mathbf{d}]]\\
&= \mathbf{a}[[\mathbf{b}\cdot\alpha \mathbf{c}]+[\mathbf{b}\cdot\beta \mathbf{d}]]\\
&= \alpha \mathbf{a}(\mathbf{b}\cdot \mathbf{c})+\beta \mathbf{a}(\mathbf{b}\cdot \mathbf{d})
\end{align*}
$$
Thus $\mathbf{a}\mathbf{b}$ is a tensor. Its components with respect to some $\{\mathbf{e}_{1},\mathbf{e}_{2},\mathbf{e}_{3}\}$ are $$
(\mathbf{a}\mathbf{b})_{ij} = (\mathbf{a}\mathbf{b})\mathbf{e}_{j}\cdot \mathbf{e}_{i} = \mathbf{a}(\mathbf{b}\cdot \mathbf{e}_{j})\cdot \mathbf{e}_{i} = b_{j}\mathbf{a}\cdot \mathbf{e}_{i} = a_{i}b_{j},
$$which has matrix form $$
\mathbf{a}\mathbf{b}=\begin{bmatrix}
a_{1}b_{1} & a_{1}b_{2} & a_{1}b_{3}  \\
a_{2}b_{1} & a_{2}b_{2} & a_{2}b_{3} \\
a_{3}b_{1} & a_{3}b_{2} & a_{3}b_{3}
\end{bmatrix}
$$
Some authors use $\mathbf{a}\otimes \mathbf{b}$ for $\mathbf{a}\mathbf{b}$. 

In particular,$$
[\mathbf{e}_{1}\mathbf{e}_{1}] = \begin{bmatrix}
1 & 0 & 0 \\
0 & 0 & 0 \\
0 & 0 & 0
\end{bmatrix},\quad [\mathbf{e}_{2}\mathbf{e}_{1}] = \begin{bmatrix}
0 & 0 & 0 \\
1 & 0 & 0 \\
0 & 0 & 0
\end{bmatrix},etc.
$$
So in general, $$
[\mathbf{e}_{i}\mathbf{e}_{j}] = \begin{cases}
1\quad \text{at}\quad ij \\
0\quad \text{otherwise}
\end{cases}
$$
Any tensor can be expressed as $$
\mathbf{T} = T_{ij}\mathbf{e}_{i}\mathbf{e}_{j} = T_{11}\mathbf{e}_{1}\mathbf{e}_{1}+T_{12}\mathbf{e}_{1}\mathbf{e}_{2}+T_{13}\mathbf{e}_{1}\mathbf{e}_{3}+T_{21}\mathbf{e}_{2}\mathbf{e}_{1}+\dots
$$
