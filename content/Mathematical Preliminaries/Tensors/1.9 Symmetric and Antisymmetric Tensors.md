A tensor $\mathbf{T}$ is symmetric if 
$$
\mathbf{T}=\mathbf{T}^T.
$$
A tensor $\mathbf{T}$ is antisymmetric if 
$$
\mathbf{T} = -\mathbf{T}^T.
$$
Any tensor can be decomposed into the sum of a symmetric and antisymmetric tensor
$$
\mathbf{T} = \mathbf{T}^S + \mathbf{T}^A,
$$
where 
$$
\mathbf{T}^S = \frac{\mathbf{T}+\mathbf{T}^T}{2} \quad \text{is symmetric},
$$
and 
$$
\mathbf{T}^A = \frac{\mathbf{T}-\mathbf{T}^T}{2} \quad \text{is antisymmetric}.
$$

Claim: This decomposition is unique. 
Proof:
Suppose $\exists$ a symmetric tensor $\mathbf{S}$ and antisymmetric tensor $\mathbf{A}$ where 
$$
\mathbf{T} = \mathbf{S}+\mathbf{A},
$$
and 
$$
	\mathbf{S}\neq \frac{\mathbf{T}+\mathbf{T}^T}{2}, \quad \mathbf{A} \neq \frac{\mathbf{T}-\mathbf{T}^T}{2}. 
$$
Taking the transpose 
$$
\mathbf{T}^T = \mathbf{S}^T-\mathbf{A}^T \Rightarrow \mathbf{A}^T = \mathbf{S}^T - \mathbf{T}^T = \mathbf{S}-\mathbf{T}^T \Rightarrow -\mathbf{A}=\mathbf{S}-\mathbf{T}^T.
$$
So we have the simultaneous equations for $\mathbf{A}$,
$$
\mathbf{A}=\mathbf{T}-\mathbf{S}
$$
$$
\mathbf{A} = -\mathbf{S}+\mathbf{T}^T
$$
i.e.
$$
\begin{align}
\mathbf{T}-\mathbf{S}&=-\mathbf{S}+\mathbf{T}^T \\
2\mathbf{S}&=\mathbf{T}+\mathbf{T}^T \\
\mathbf{S}&=\frac{\mathbf{T}+\mathbf{T}^T}{2}
\end{align}
$$
Which is a contradiction. Hence $\mathbf{T}^S = \mathbf{S}$ is unique. The decomposition is then unique by symmetry.
