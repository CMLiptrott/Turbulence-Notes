Let $\{\mathbf{e}_{1},\mathbf{e}_{2},\mathbf{e}_{3}\}$ and $\{\mathbf{e}_{1}',\mathbf{e}_{2}',\mathbf{e}_{3}'\}$ be unit vectors corresponding to two Cartesian coordinate systems. Clearly $\{\mathbf{e}_{1},\mathbf{e}_{2},\mathbf{e}_{3}\}$ can be made to correspond with $\{\mathbf{e}_{1}',\mathbf{e}_{2}',\mathbf{e}_{3}'\}$ through either a rigid body rotation (if both bases are same handed) or a rotation followed by a reflection (if different handed). Then  $\{\mathbf{e}_{1},\mathbf{e}_{2},\mathbf{e}_{3}\}$ and $\{\mathbf{e}_{1}',\mathbf{e}_{2}',\mathbf{e}_{3}'\}$ are related by an orthogonal tensor $\mathbf{Q}$ through the equations 
$$
\mathbf{e}_{i} = \mathbf{Q}\mathbf{e}_{i}' = Q_{mi}e_{m}' 
$$
i.e. 
$$
\begin{bmatrix}
e_{1} \\
e_{2} \\
e_{3}  
\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}
Q_{11} & Q_{12} & Q_{13} \\
Q_{21} & Q_{22} & Q_{23} \\ 
Q_{31} & Q_{32} & Q_{33} 
\end{bmatrix}
\begin{bmatrix}
e_{1}' \\
e_{2}' \\
e_{3}'
\end{bmatrix}
$$
$\mathbf{Q}$ is orthogonal so 
$$
\mathbf{Q}\mathbf{Q}^T = \mathbf{Q}^T\mathbf{Q}=\mathbf{I}
$$
In index notation,
$$
Q_{im}Q_{jm} = Q_{im}Q^T_{mj} = \delta_{ij},
$$
and 
$$
Q_{mi}Q_{mj} = Q^T_{im}Q_{mj} \delta_{ij}.
$$
Also note that 
$$
Q_{mi} = \mathbf{e}_{m} \cdot \mathbf{Q}\mathbf{e}_{i} = \mathbf{e}_{m}\cdot \mathbf{e}_{i}' = \cos(\mathbf{e}_{m},\mathbf{e}_{i}')
$$
Now, consider any vector $\mathbf{a}$. The components with respect to $\{\mathbf{e}_{1},\mathbf{e}_{2},\mathbf{e}_{3}\}$ and $\{\mathbf{e}_{1}',\mathbf{e}_{2}',\mathbf{e}_{3}'\}$ are $a_{i} = \mathbf{a}\cdot \mathbf{e}_{i}$ and $a_{i}'=\mathbf{a}\cdot \mathbf{e}_{i}'$. Since $a_{i}'=\mathbf{e}_{i}'\cdot \mathbf{a} = Q_{mi}\mathbf{e}_{m}\cdot \mathbf{a}$, we have 
$$
a_{i}'=Q_{mi}a_{m}.
$$
In matrix form 
$$
\begin{bmatrix}
a_{1}' \\
a_{2}' \\
a_{3}'
\end{bmatrix}_{\mathbf{e}_{i}'}=\begin{bmatrix}
Q_{11} & Q_{21} & Q_{31} \\
Q_{12} & Q_{22} & Q_{32}  \\
Q_{13} & Q_{23} & Q_{33} 
\end{bmatrix}_{\mathbf{e}_{i}}\begin{bmatrix}
a_{1} \\
a_{2} \\
a_{3}
\end{bmatrix}_{\mathbf{e}_{i}}
$$
or
$$
 [\mathbf{a}]'=[\mathbf{Q}]^T[\mathbf{a}].
$$
This is the **transformation law** relating components of the same vector with respect to different rectangular Cartesian unit bases. 

Important: $[\mathbf{a}]'=[\mathbf{a}]_{\mathbf{e}_{i}'}$ and $[\mathbf{a}]=[\mathbf{a}]_{\mathbf{e}_{i}}$. That is, transformation law is not the matrix equation for $\mathbf{a}'=\mathbf{Q}^T\mathbf{a}$. Both $[\mathbf{a}]'$ and $[\mathbf{a}]$ are matrices of the same vector, whereas $\mathbf{a}'$ is the transformed vector of $\mathbf{a}$ through $\mathbf{Q}^T$!  Think about this visually.
