Primary test for determining whether a force is conservative or not is this: Let the force act on a particle that moves along any closed path. The force is conservative only if the total energy it transfers to and from the particle during the round trip along this and any other closed path is zero. 

**The net work done by a conservative force on a particle moving around any closed path is zero.** 

**The work done on a particle by a conservative force between two points does not depend on the path taken**

This can be expressed as 
$$
 W_{ab,1} = W_{ab,2}
$$
where the start and end points are $a$ and $b$ and the subscripts $1,2$ refer to different paths. 
Proof:
Take path 1 from $a$ to $b$, then take path 2 back from $b$ to $a$. Let the work done from $a$ to $b$ along path 1 be $W_{ab,1}$ and from $b$ to $a$ along path 2 be $W_{ab,2}$.  The force is conservative so the total work done is zero. Then 
$$
W_{ab,1} + W_{ba,2} = 0
$$ or 
$$
W_{ab,1} = -W_{ba,2}
$$
But the work done along path 2 from $a$ to $b$ is the negative of the work from $b$ to $a$. Hence
$$
W_{ab,2} = W_{ba,2}
$$
and we recover 
$$
W_{ab,1} = W_{ab,2}
$$
