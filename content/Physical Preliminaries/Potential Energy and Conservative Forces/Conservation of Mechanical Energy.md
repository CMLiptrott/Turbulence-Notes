<p style="text-transform: uppercase; font-size: 0.8em; font-weight: bold; margin-bottom: 0;text-align: center;">Mechanical Energy</p>
The mechanical energy $E_{\text{mec}}$ of a system is the sum of its potential energy $U$ and the kinetic energy $K$ if the objects within it

$$
E_{\text{mec}}=K+U
$$
Consider only conservative forces in the following. When a conservative force does work $W$ on an object within the system the force transfers energy between the kinetic energy $K$ of the object and the potential energy $U$ of the system. From the work energy theorem
$$
\Delta K = W
$$
and from [[Work and Potential Energy|work-potential energy relationship]] 
$$
-\Delta U = W
$$
So
$$
\Delta K = -\Delta U
$$
**So these energies increase as much as the other decreases.** 
In other works 
$$
\left(\text{Sum of $K$ and $U$ for  any state of the system}\right) = \left(\text{Sum of $K$ and $U$ for any other state of the system}\right)
$$
when the system is isolated and only conservative forces act on the objects in the system. This result is the **principle of conservation of mechanical energy**
<p style="text-transform: uppercase; font-size: 0.8em; font-weight: bold; margin-bottom: 0;text-align: center;">Principle of Conservation of Mechanical Energy</p>
In an isolated system where only conservative forces cause energy changes, the kinetic energy and potential energy can change, but their sum, the mechanical energy $E_{\text{mec}}$ cannot change. That is 
$$
\Delta E_{\text{mec}} = \Delta K + \Delta U = 0
$$
