
<p style="text-transform: uppercase; font-size: 0.8em; font-weight: bold; margin-bottom: 0;text-align: center;">Potential Energy</p>
The energy that can be associated with the configuration (arrangement) of a system of objects that exert forces on one another. 
<p style="text-transform: uppercase; font-size: 0.8em; font-weight: bold; margin-bottom: 0;text-align: center;">Gravitational Potential Energy</p>
The energy associated with the state of separation between two objects that attract each other by the gravitational force.

<p style="text-transform: uppercase; font-size: 0.8em; font-weight: bold; margin-bottom: 0;text-align: center;">Elastic Potential Energy</p>
The energy associated with the state of compression or extension of an elastic object. 

<p style="text-transform: uppercase; font-size: 0.8em; font-weight: bold; margin-bottom: 0;text-align: center;">Conservative Forces I</p>
The net work done by a conservative force on a particle moving around any closed path is zero.

<p style="text-transform: uppercase; font-size: 0.8em; font-weight: bold; margin-bottom: 0;text-align: center;">Conservative Forces II</p>
The work done on a particle by a conservative force between two points does not depend on the path taken

<p style="text-transform: uppercase; font-size: 0.8em; font-weight: bold; margin-bottom: 0;text-align: center;">Mechanical Energy</p>
The mechanical energy $E_{\text{mec}}$ of a system is the sum of its potential energy $U$ and the kinetic energy $K$ if the objects within it

$$
E_{\text{mec}}=K+U
$$

<p style="text-transform: uppercase; font-size: 0.8em; font-weight: bold; margin-bottom: 0;text-align: center;">Principle of Conservation of Mechanical Energy</p>
In an isolated system where only conservative forces cause energy changes, the kinetic energy and potential energy can change, but their sum, the mechanical energy $E_{\text{mec}}$ cannot change. That is 
$$
\Delta E_{\text{mec}} = \Delta K + \Delta U = 0
$$
