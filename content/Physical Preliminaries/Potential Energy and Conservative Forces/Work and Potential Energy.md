Throw a tomato in the air. The work done by the gravitational force on the tomato is negative. The gravitational force transfers energy **from** the kinetic energy of the tomato. Where does the energy go? The gravitational force transfers energy **to** the gravitational potential energy of the tomato-Earth system. 

The change in gravitational potential energy $\Delta U$ is defined as the negative of the work done (on the tomato by the gravitational force)
$$
\Delta U = -W
$$
The key elements of this situation are:
1) The system consists of two or more objects
2) A force acts between a particle-like object (tomato) in the system and the rest of the system
3) When the configuration changes, the force foes work $W_{1}$ on the object, transferring energy between the kinetic energy $K$ of the object and some other type of energy in the system. 
4) When the configuration change is reversed, the force reverses the energy transfer, doing work $W_{2}$ in the process

In a situation where $W_{1}=-W_{2}$ is always true, the other type of energy is a potential energy and the force is said to be a **conservative force**. The gravitational force is conservative. 

A force that is not conservative is called a **nonconservative force**. The kinetic drag force is nonconservative. Consider an object sliding across a frictional surface. A kinetic frictional force does negative work on the block, slowing the block by transferring energy from its kinetic energy to its thermal energy. We know from experiment that this energy transfer cannot be reversed (thermal energy cannot be transferred back to kinetic energy of the block by the kinetic frictional force). Thus although we have a system, a force that acts between parts of the system, and a transfer of energy by the force, the force is not conservative. Therefore thermal energy is not a potential energy.
