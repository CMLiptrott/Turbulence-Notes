The with of the [[1.3.1 Maxwellian Distribution|one-dimensional Maxwellian distribution]] is characterised by $T$, which we call the temperature. To see the meaning of $T$, compute the average kinetic energy of particles in this distribution:
$$
E_{av} = \frac{\int_{\infty}^{\infty} \frac{1}{2}m u^2 f(u)du}{\int_{\infty}^{\infty} f(u)du}
$$

We divide by the number density because $f(u)$ isn't normalised to 1, so division by $n$ ensures we get the average kinetic energy per particle. Now define
$$
v_{th}=(2KT /m)^{1/2}\quad \text{and}\quad y=\frac{u}{v_{th}}
$$
Then 
$$
 f(u) = A\exp\left( -\frac{u^2}{v_{th}^2} \right) = A\exp\left( -y^2 \right) 
$$
So the average kinetic energy is re-written as 
$$
E_{av} = \frac{\frac{1}{2}m A v_{th}^2\int_{\infty}^{\infty} \exp(-y^2)y^2dy}{\int_{\infty}^{\infty}  \exp(-y^2)dy}
$$
After integrating by parts the numerator is 
$$
\frac{1}{2} \int_{\infty}^{\infty}  \exp(-y^2)dy
$$
So we get 
$$
E_{av} = \frac{\frac{1}{4}mAv_{th}^3}{A v_{th}} = \frac{1}{4}mv_{th}^2 = \frac{1}{2} K T
$$
So the average kinetic energy is $\frac{1}{2}KT$.

Extending this to three dimensions we get 
$$
 E_{av} = \frac{3}{2}KT
$$
where each dimension contributes equally due to the isotropic nature of the Maxwellian distribution. In general $E_{av}$ equals $\frac{1}{2}KT$ per degree of freedom.
