Plasma can have several temperatures at once. The ions and electrons can have separate Maxwellian distributions with different temperatures $T_{i}$ and $T_{e}$. This happens if the collisions between ions (electrons) among themselves is greater than the collisions between ions and electrons. 

Each species can then be in its own thermal equilibrium, but the plasma may not last long enough for the two temperatures to equalise. 

When there is a magnetic field $\mathbf{B}$, even a single species can have two temperatures. Forces acting on an ion along $\mathbf{B}$ are different than those acting on the ion perpendicular to $\mathbf{B}$ (due to the Lorentz force).  The components of velocity perpendicular and parallel to $\mathbf{B}$ may belong to different Maxwellian distributions with temperatures $T_{\perp}$ and $T_{\parallel}$.
